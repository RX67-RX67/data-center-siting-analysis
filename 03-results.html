Model Interpretation

SHAP Framework

We use SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) with TreeExplainer for exact SHAP values — not approximations. SHAP assigns each feature a contribution to the model’s output for each individual county, with the sum of contributions equaling the difference between the county’s prediction and the global mean prediction.

f(x) = \phi_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{p} \phi_j

where \phi_0 is the base value (mean prediction) and \phi_j is feature j’s SHAP contribution. Positive SHAP → pushes prediction up; negative SHAP → pushes down.

All 3,138 county SHAP values were pre-computed and stored for efficient lookup.

Mean |SHAP| across all counties ranks features by their average contribution to the presence probability. This is the model’s answer to: “What structural factors most determine whether a county attracts any data center?”

Key Interpretive Findings

1. Power Infrastructure as the Entry Gate

clean_energy_jobs (rank 1, S1) and grid_infrastructure_jobs (rank 2, S1) are the two strongest presence predictors. These are not directly measuring power availability but rather the depth of the power sector workforce — a proxy for grid capacity, interconnection maturity, and the density of utility infrastructure needed to serve a hyperscale campus.

This aligns with industry reporting: power access (not just price) is consistently cited as the top constraint on new data center development.

2. The Agglomeration Signal in wage_information

wage_information — the average wage in information-sector occupations — rises from rank 4 in Stage 1 to rank 1 in Stage 2. This is the clearest agglomeration signal in the model:

  • At the presence stage, IT labor is important but secondary to power infrastructure
  • At the scale stage, it becomes the dominant factor — established markets attract more facilities because they’ve already built deep IT talent pools

This is consistent with the “tech cluster” dynamic: once a few large operators anchor in a metro, the labor market deepens, which attracts more operators, reinforcing the cluster.

3. Geographic Confounding in Hazard Risk Indices

NRI hazard risk indices (lightning, hurricane, tornado, earthquake) appear in the top 8 Stage 1 predictors but fall to ranks 20–30 in Stage 2. This pattern is diagnostic of geographic confounding:

The existing US data center market is concentrated in Virginia (high lightning risk), Texas (hurricane/tornado risk), and Georgia (coastal storm risk). The model learns this geographic correlation in Stage 1. But once we condition on positive counties in Stage 2, the hazard indices lose predictive power — confirming they are proxies for where the market already is, not genuine attractors.

Important

Policy implication: Hazard risk should not be used as a site selection criterion based on this model. Counties with lower hazard risk are not inherently less attractive — the observed correlation reflects historical accident, not structural causation.

4. Electricity Cost Sensitivity at Scale

commercial_price rises from rank 9 (S1) to rank 4 (S2). Cost sensitivity is a scale-dependent phenomenon: small operators may accept above-market power costs if other factors (labor, land, connectivity) are favorable, but hyperscale campuses consuming 100s of MW are acutely sensitive to cents-per-kWh differences.

## Stage 1 vs Stage 2 — Rank Shift Analysis

The two stages ask fundamentally different questions. Comparing feature ranks between
them reveals which factors drive *entry* vs *clustering*.

```{python}
#| echo: false
#| fig-cap: "Feature rank in Stage 1 vs Stage 2 (by mean |SHAP|). Lines show rank movement between stages."
rank_s1 = pd.Series(np.abs(sv1).mean(axis=0), index=s1_feats).rank(ascending=False).astype(int)
rank_s2 = pd.Series(np.abs(sv2).mean(axis=0), index=s2_feats).rank(ascending=False).astype(int)

top_feats = rank_s1.nsmallest(15).index.tolist()
rank_df = pd.DataFrame({
    "feature": top_feats,
    "Stage 1 Rank": rank_s1[top_feats].values,
    "Stage 2 Rank": rank_s2[top_feats].values,
})
rank_df["shift"] = rank_df["Stage 1 Rank"] - rank_df["Stage 2 Rank"]
rank_df["color"] = rank_df["shift"].apply(lambda x: "#43a047" if x < -3 else ("#ef5350" if x > 3 else "#90a4ae"))
rank_df = rank_df.sort_values("Stage 1 Rank")

fig = go.Figure()
for _, row in rank_df.iterrows():
    fig.add_trace(go.Scatter(
        x=[1, 2], y=[row["Stage 1 Rank"], row["Stage 2 Rank"]],
        mode="lines+markers+text",
        line=dict(color=row["color"], width=2),
        marker=dict(size=8, color=row["color"]),
        text=["", row["feature"]],
        textposition="middle right",
        showlegend=False,
        hovertemplate=f"{row['feature']}<br>S1 rank: {row['Stage 1 Rank']}<br>S2 rank: {row['Stage 2 Rank']}<extra></extra>",
    ))
fig.update_layout(
    xaxis=dict(tickvals=[1, 2], ticktext=["Stage 1<br>(Presence)", "Stage 2<br>(Scale)"],
               showgrid=False, range=[0.8, 2.8]),
    yaxis=dict(autorange="reversed", showgrid=True, gridcolor="#eee",
               title="Rank (1 = most important)"),
    plot_bgcolor="white", paper_bgcolor="white",
    margin=dict(l=10, r=180, t=20, b=20), height=480,
)
fig.show()
```

```{python}
#| echo: false
def county_profile(county_key, label):
    row = ranking[ranking["county_key"] == county_key].iloc[0]
    sv1_row = shap_df.loc[county_key, s1_cols].values.astype(float)
    sv2_row = shap_df.loc[county_key, s2_cols].values.astype(float)

    idx1 = np.argsort(np.abs(sv1_row))[-8:][::-1]
    idx2 = np.argsort(np.abs(sv2_row))[-8:][::-1]

    profile = pd.DataFrame({
        "Stage 1 Feature": [s1_feats[i] for i in idx1],
        "S1 SHAP": [sv1_row[i] for i in idx1],
        "Stage 2 Feature": [s2_feats[i] for i in idx2],
        "S2 SHAP": [sv2_row[i] for i in idx2],
    })
    return row, profile

for ck, lbl in [
    ("Virginia||Loudoun County",   "#1 Loudoun County, VA"),
    ("Texas||Travis County",       "#4 Travis County, TX"),
    ("North Carolina||New Hanover County", "#80 New Hanover County, NC (zero DC)"),
]:
    row, profile = county_profile(ck, lbl)
    print(f"\n{'='*60}")
    print(f"{lbl}")
    print(f"  DCs: {row['num_datacenters']:.1f}  |  P(presence): {row['p_presence']:.3f}  "
          f"|  Score: {row['attractiveness']:.3f}")
```
