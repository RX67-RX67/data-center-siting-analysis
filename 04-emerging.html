Emerging Markets

What Is an Emerging Market?

An emerging market in this context is a county that:

  1. Has zero current data centers (no incumbents to compete with)
  2. Has high structural attractiveness — the model finds the prerequisite conditions for DC activity already in place

These are first-mover opportunities: the infrastructure is there, the labor exists, the grid capacity is present — but no operator has yet planted a flag. The model identifies these by the gap between structural readiness and realized investment.

Investment Thesis Interpretation

Three distinct opportunity profiles emerge from the top emerging markets:

Profile 1 — Metro Adjacency (e.g., New Hanover NC, Monmouth NJ) Counties adjacent to established DC markets that have absorbed surrounding infrastructure development (grid, fiber, labor) without yet attracting operators. These are likely to see activity as primary markets become capacity-constrained.

Profile 2 — Underserved Regional Hubs (e.g., Mercer NJ, Clackamas OR) Mid-size metros with strong structural foundations but no historical DC presence. Often offer lower land costs than primary markets with comparable grid and labor depth.

Profile 3 — Policy-Driven Opportunities (e.g., Sussex DE, Calvert MD) Counties in states with favorable data center tax incentives and regulatory environments. The policy signal (policy_direction_score) contributes positively to attractiveness even where other structural factors are more moderate.

Note

Limitation: This model captures structural conditions but cannot account for announced but not-yet-built capacity, utility queue positions, or off-market land deals. Real investment decisions require augmenting these signals with proprietary market intelligence.
