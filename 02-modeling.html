Modeling Approach

Why Not a Simple Regression?

The target variable — county-level data center count — violates the assumptions of standard regression in two compounding ways:

  1. Zero-inflation: 75.6% of counties have exactly zero data centers. A regression trained on all counties conflates two fundamentally different groups: counties that could attract a DC but haven’t, and counties that are structurally unsuitable.

  2. Right skew + overdispersion: Among positive counties, counts range from <1 to 304, with variance far exceeding the mean. Standard OLS residuals would be severely heteroscedastic.

These two problems motivate different solutions, which led us to evaluate two model families.

Option 1 — Tweedie Regression

The Tweedie distribution with power parameter p ∈ (1, 2) is a compound Poisson-Gamma that naturally handles zero-inflation and overdispersion in a single model. It directly models E[y] with a log link, treating zeros as a structural outcome.

Key hyperparameter: the Tweedie power p, tuned via cross-validation.

Best p = 1.3 (closer to Poisson than Gamma), reflecting that the zero mass is the dominant challenge over heavy-tail scaling.

Option 2 — Two-Part Hurdle Model ✓

The hurdle model explicitly separates the two questions:

E[y] = \underbrace{P(y > 0)}_{\text{Stage 1}} \times \underbrace{E[y \mid y > 0]}_{\text{Stage 2}}

Stage 1 — Binary Classifier (LightGBM, is_unbalance=True): Predicts whether a county has any data center presence. Trained on all 3,138 counties.

Stage 2 — Tweedie Regressor (LightGBM, Tweedie p tuned): Predicts the expected count given presence. Trained only on the 767 counties with at least one data center.

This decomposition is both statistically principled and interpretively richer: we get separate SHAP explanations for the presence decision and the scale decision.

from lightgbm import LGBMClassifier, LGBMRegressor

# Stage 1 — presence
stage1 = LGBMClassifier(
    n_estimators=1000, learning_rate=0.05, num_leaves=63,
    is_unbalance=True,          # handles 76% zero class imbalance
    early_stopping_rounds=50,
)
stage1.fit(X_train, y_binary_train, eval_set=[(X_val, y_binary_val)])

# Stage 2 — scale (positive counties only)
stage2 = LGBMRegressor(
    objective="tweedie", tweedie_variance_power=best_p,
    n_estimators=1000, learning_rate=0.05, num_leaves=63,
    early_stopping_rounds=50,
)
stage2.fit(X_pos_train, y_pos_train, eval_set=[(X_pos_val, y_pos_val)])

Hyperparameter Tuning

Both stages used Optuna for Bayesian hyperparameter optimization (50 trials each), with 5-fold cross-validation as the objective. Key parameters tuned: num_leaves, learning_rate, min_child_samples, subsample, colsample_bytree, and reg_lambda. Stage 2 also tuned the Tweedie power p.

Stage 2 best p = 1.8 (closer to Gamma), reflecting that among positive counties, the count distribution is heavy-tailed — a handful of markets dominate the landscape.

Model Comparison

Performance comparison across five metrics. Option 2 (Hurdle) wins on four of five.

Option 1 — Tweedie Option 2 — Hurdle ✓ Winner
Metric
Tweedie Deviance ↓ 1.756 1.965 Tweedie
MAE log1p ↓ 0.312 0.279 Hurdle ✓
RMSE log1p ↓ 0.474 0.524 Tweedie
AUC ↑ 0.807 0.833 Hurdle ✓
Median AE ↓ 0.185 0.052 Hurdle ✓

The hurdle model wins on the metrics that matter most for practical application:

  • AUC +2.6 pp (0.807 → 0.833): better discrimination of DC-present vs absent counties
  • Median AE 3.6× lower (0.185 → 0.052): far more accurate predictions at the median, meaning the model is right more often for typical counties
  • MAE (log1p) improvement: better average accuracy in log space

The Tweedie model wins on Tweedie Deviance (expected, since it directly optimizes that objective) and RMSE (driven by a few large-count outliers that the Tweedie handles better in absolute terms). For siting decisions, discrimination and median accuracy are more actionable than extreme-tail RMSE.

Why is_unbalance=True in Stage 1?

The binary classification target in Stage 1 is 76.2% negative (no DC present). Without correction, LightGBM would learn a trivial solution of predicting “no DC” for most counties. is_unbalance=True automatically adjusts class weights by the inverse frequency ratio, giving the minority class (has DC) proportionally more influence during training.

An alternative would be scale_pos_weight, but is_unbalance handles the ratio automatically and showed equivalent results in our cross-validation experiments.
